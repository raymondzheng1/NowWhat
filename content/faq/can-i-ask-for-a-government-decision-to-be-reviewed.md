If a Victorian government department, council or agency made a decision you disagree
with, you may be able to have it reviewed. Whether you can, and how, depends on the law
the decision was made under.

## Can it go to VCAT?

VCAT can only review a decision if the Act it was made under gives VCAT that power. So
the first thing to check is your decision letter. It should tell you if you can apply to
VCAT, and the time limit. If the letter does not mention a review right, it may not be
one VCAT can hear — get advice rather than assume.

## Ask for the reasons in writing

You can ask the decision-maker for a written statement of reasons. Ask within 28 days of
the decision. This helps you understand the decision, and it can also move the start of
your time to apply to VCAT to when you get the reasons.

## Time limits

The time limit to apply to VCAT is often 28 days, but the Act that applies can set a
different, sometimes shorter, limit. Check the time limit on your letter and act early.

## If a decision can't be reviewed

If your decision can't go to VCAT, you can still complain to the Victorian Ombudsman
about unfair treatment by a Victorian government body. The Ombudsman is free, but cannot
overturn or change a decision.
