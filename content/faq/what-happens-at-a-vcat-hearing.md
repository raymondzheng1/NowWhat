## What is VCAT?

VCAT stands for the Victorian Civil and Administrative Tribunal. It has a special list for renting cases. It is called the **Residential Tenancies list**. VCAT can decide if a notice to vacate is valid.

## What can VCAT look at?

VCAT can check things like:

- Whether the notice to vacate was on the correct form and gave a valid reason
- Whether the notice period was long enough for the reason given
- Whether the reason on the notice is a lawful and genuine one
- Whether the notice was given because you asked for repairs or stood up for your rights

If you challenge a notice early, there are time limits. So it helps to act as soon as you can. If you miss the early window, you may still be able to argue the notice is not valid. You can do this at the possession hearing. You can also ask for more time.

## What should I bring?

Useful things to have ready include:

- The notice or letter itself, so the date, form, and reason can be checked
- Your rental agreement (lease) and any condition report
- Your rent payment records
- Messages with the rental provider or agent
- Photos of the condition of the place and any repairs needed
- The envelope or postmark showing when the notice arrived

## Where can I get help?

Free help is available before and during a VCAT hearing:

- **Tenants Victoria** — free advice for Victorian renters on the Rental Support Line **(03) 9416 2577**
- **Victoria Legal Aid** — free legal help and duty lawyers on **(1300 792 387)**
- **Rental Dispute Resolution Victoria (RDRV)** — free help to resolve disputes, on **(1300 017 378)**
